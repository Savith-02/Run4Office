<p>The city of <a href="/Raleigh,_North_Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a>, held a general election for mayor on October 8, 2019. The filing deadline for this election was July 19, 2019.
</p><p><a href="https://ballotpedia.org/Mary-Ann_Baldwin">Mary-Ann Baldwin</a>  won election  in the  general election for Mayor of Raleigh. </p>
<p>Because no candidate received a majority of the votes in the general election, a general runoff election scheduled for November 5, 2019, was possible. <a href="/Charles_Francis">Charles Francis</a>, the runner-up in the general election, announced on October 11, 2019, that he would not call for the runoff, meaning Baldwin was declared the winner. Francis stated, "After reviewing the results and analyzing a runoff race, I have concluded that the path to a runoff victory to re-connect with my voters and reach other voters concerned about our city would require an additional several hundred thousand dollars—more resources than available for an election just three weeks away. Therefore, though there was no clear mandate in the Mayor’s race for any candidate, I will not call for a runoff."
</p>

<h2><span>Mayoral partisanship</span></h2>
<p>Once mayors elected in 2019 assumed office, the mayors of 65 of the country's 100 largest cities were affiliated with the <a href="/Democratic_Party">Democratic Party</a>. Out of the 31 mayoral elections that were held in 2019 in the 100 largest cities, five partisan changes occurred. Democrats gained three mayorships: two previously held by Republicans and one previously held by an independent. Republicans won one office held by an unaffiliated mayor, and one office where the incumbent's partisan affiliation was unknown.
</p><p>In the elections in <a href="/Mayoral_election_in_Phoenix,_Arizona_(2018-2019)">Phoenix, Arizona</a> and <a href="/Mayoral_election_in_Wichita,_Kansas_(2019)">Wichita, Kansas</a>, Democrats won seats with Republican incumbents. In Wichita, Democrat <a href="/Brandon_Whipple">Brandon Whipple</a> defeated Republican incumbent <a href="/Jeff_Longwell">Jeff Longwell</a>. In <a>Raleigh, North Carolina</a>, a Democrat won a seat previously held by an independent. In <a href="/Mayoral_election_in_Aurora,_Colorado_(2019)">Aurora, Colorado</a>, a Republican succeeded an unaffiliated mayor. In <a href="/Garland,_Texas">Garland, Texas</a>, a Republican succeeded a mayor with unknown party affiliation. Incumbents did not seek re-election in Phoenix, Raleigh, Aurora, or Garland.

<h2><span>About the city</span></h2>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><i>See also: <a href="/Raleigh,_North_Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a></i></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/Raleigh,_North_Carolina">Raleigh</a> is a city in <a href="/Wake_County,_North_Carolina">Wake County</a>, <a href="/North_Carolina">North Carolina</a>. As of 2010, its population was 403,892.
</p>
<h3><span>City government</span></h3>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><i>See also: <a href="/Council-manager_government">Council-manager government</a></i></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>The city of Raleigh uses a council-manager system. In this form of municipal government, an elected city council—which includes the mayor and serves as the city's primary legislative body—appoints a chief executive called a city manager to oversee day-to-day municipal operations and implement the council's policy and legislative initiatives.
</p>

<h3><span>Partisan data</span></h3>
<p>The information in this section was current as of May 7, 2019
</p><p><big><b>Presidential voting pattern</b></big>
</p>
<ul><li>North Carolina <a href="/Presidential_voting_trends_in_North_Carolina">voted Republican</a> in five out of the six presidential elections between 2000 and 2020.</li></ul>
<p><big><b>Congressional delegation</b></big>
</p>
<ul><li>Following the <a href="/North_Carolina_elections,_2018">2018 elections</a>, both <a href="/United_States_congressional_delegations_from_North_Carolina">U.S. Senators from North Carolina</a> were Republicans.</li>
<li>North Carolina had three Democratic and eight Republican U.S. Representatives, and two seats were vacant.</li></ul>
<p><big><b>State executives</b></big>
</p>
<ul><li>Democrats held four and Republicans held six of North Carolina's 20 <a href="/North_Carolina_state_executive_offices">state executive offices</a>. Elections for the other offices are nonpartisan.</li>
<li>North Carolina's governor was Democrat <a href="/Roy_Cooper">Roy Cooper</a>.</li></ul>
<p><big><b>State legislature</b></big>
</p>
<ul><li>Republicans controlled the <a href="/North_Carolina_State_Senate">North Carolina State Senate</a> with a 29-21 majority.</li>
<li>Republicans controlled the <a href="/North_Carolina_House_of_Representatives">North Carolina House of Representatives</a> with a 65-54 majority.</li></ul>

<p>In the 2016 presidential election, <a href="/Donald_Trump">Donald Trump</a> (R) won North Carolina with 49.8 percent of the vote. <a href="/Hillary_Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> (D) received 46.2 percent. In presidential elections between 1792 and 2016, North Carolina voted Democratic 53.5 percent of the time and Republican 25 percent of the time. In the five presidential elections between 2000 and 2016, North Carolina voted Republican all five times with the exception of the 2008 presidential election.
