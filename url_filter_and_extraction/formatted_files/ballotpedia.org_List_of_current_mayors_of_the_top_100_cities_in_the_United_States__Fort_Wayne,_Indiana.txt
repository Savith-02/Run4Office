In this form of municipal government, the city council serves as the city's primary legislative body and the mayor serves as the city's chief executive.</p><h3><span>Mayor</span></h3><dl><dd><dl><dd><i>See also:<a>List of current mayors of the top 100 cities in the United States</a></i></dd></dl></dd></dl><p>The mayor serves as the city's chief executive. He or she is responsible for proposing a budget, signing legislation into law, appointing departmental directors and committee members, and overseeing the city's day-to-day operations. The mayor also represents the city on the state, national, and international levels. The current Mayor of Fort Wayne is<a>Sharon Tucker</a>(D). Tucker assumed office in 2024.</p><h3><span>City council</span></h3><dl><dd><dl><dd><i>See also:<a>List of current city council officials of the top 100 cities in the United States</a></i></dd></dl></dd></dl><p>The Fort Wayne Common Council is the city's primary legislative body. It is responsible for approving and adopting the city budget, levying taxes, and making or amending city laws and policies.</p><p>The city council is made up of nine members. Three members are elected at large, while the other six are elected by the city's six districts.</p><p>The widget below automatically displays information about city council meetings. The budget process begins annually in June when all city departments draft budgets for the next fiscal year. The proposed department budgets are reviewed by the city's Division of Finance and Administration before the mayor presents the city budget to the common council in September. The common council holds hearings to allow for public input in the budget process. By state law, the common council must adopt the budget by November 1.</p><h3><span>Fiscally standardized cities data</span></h3><p>The fiscally standardized cities (FiSC) data below was compiled by the<a>Lincoln Institute of Land Policy</a>to make municipal budgets comparable across cities in the United States.</p><div><table><tbody><tr><td>“</td><td><p>FiSCs are constructed by adding revenues and expenditures of each central city municipal government to a portion of the revenues and expenditures of overlying governments, including counties, independent school districts, and special districts.
